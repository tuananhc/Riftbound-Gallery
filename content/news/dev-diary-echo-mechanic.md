Every mechanic starts as a problem. Echo started as this one: *how do we reward players for building spell-dense decks without making them feel punished for playing creatures?*

## The First Attempt

Our first prototype was called *Resonance*. When you cast your second spell in a turn, Resonance triggers on any card in play that has it. Simple enough — but in testing it warped deck-building completely. Suddenly there was no reason to run anything other than two-spell turns, and games started to feel formulaic.

We shelved it.

## The Pivot to Echo

Six months later, a junior designer brought in a riff on the idea: what if Echo was a *cast trigger* rather than a *passive*? You choose to cast a spell, and if you've already cast one this turn, the Echo effect resolves.

The key difference: Echo is a reward, not a requirement. You're not penalised for not achieving it. It's there for the turns when you've got the resources to go wide.

## Why It Almost Didn't Ship

Echo spent three months in limbo because of one card: an early version of what became *The Rift Walker*. The original design let you Echo for free once per turn — essentially giving you a permanent bonus. It broke the economy of the mechanic entirely.

The fix was simple once we saw it: Echo should always cost something, even if that cost is small. The Rift Walker became the version you see in Set 3, and Echo shipped.

## What's Next

We're already exploring Echo in Set 4. Early designs suggest it plays beautifully with the new keyword we're testing — but that's a story for another diary.
